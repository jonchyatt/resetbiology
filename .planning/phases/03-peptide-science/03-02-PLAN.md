Study ghkcu-1: PMID 3169264, Maquart FX, Pickart L et al. 1988, FEBS Lett

- journal: "FEBS Letters"
- year: 1988
- summary: Foundational study demonstrating GHK-copper complex dose-dependently stimulates collagen synthesis in human fibroblast cultures, with effects beginning at picomolar concentration and peaking at 10^-9 M, proposing wound-site release as natural mechanism.
- keyFindings: [dose-dependent stimulation of collagen synthesis in human fibroblasts, effect begins at 10^-12 M (picomolar) — active at extremely low concentrations, peak effect at 10^-9 M independent of cell number changes, proposed mechanism: released by proteases at wound sites for local healing, in vitro human fibroblast study — topical/systemic human clinical trials limited]
- relevance: Foundational collagen synthesis evidence for GHK-Cu. Collagen type I and III are primary tendon components — connecting to tendon repair chain in Exercise Science section.

- keyFindings: [GHK levels decline ~200 ng/mL at 20 to ~80 ng/mL at 60, accelerates wound healing and skin repair with copper, stimulates collagen/elastin/glycosaminoglycan synthesis, modulates 4000+ human genes, improved skin elasticity/reduced wrinkle depth in cosmetic studies — systemic human RCTs limited]

**DSIP (2 studies):**

Study dsip-1: PMID 7028502, Schneider-Helmert D, Schoenenberger GA 1981, Experientia
- title: "The influence of synthetic DSIP (delta-sleep-inducing-peptide) on disturbed human sleep"
- authors: "Schneider-Helmert D, Schoenenberger GA"
- journal: "Experientia"
- year: 1981
- summary: Early clinical study in six chronic insomniacs showing IV DSIP produced longer sleep duration, higher sleep quality with fewer interruptions, and slightly more REM sleep with no daytime sedation.
- keyFindings: [longer sleep duration and improved quality in chronic insomniacs, fewer sleep interruptions with slightly more REM sleep, no daytime sedation — normalizing rather than sedating effect, small study (n=6) intravenous administration only — not replicated in large RCTs, suggests physiological sleep-normalizing rather than hypnotic mechanism]

Study dsip-2: PMID 3368469, Iyer KS et al. 1988, Proc Natl Acad Sci USA
- title: "Evidence for a role of delta sleep-inducing peptide in slow-wave sleep and sleep-related growth hormone release in the rat"
- authors: "Iyer KS, Marks GA, Kastin AJ, McCann SM"
- journal: "Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences USA"
- year: 1988
- summary: Mechanistic study showing DSIP antiserum injected into rat brains blocked both compensatory slow-wave sleep increase and growth hormone secretion during recovery sleep — causal evidence that endogenous DSIP is necessary for sleep-related GH release.
- keyFindings: [DSIP antiserum blocked sleep-deprivation-induced increase in slow-wave sleep, same antiserum blocked compensatory GH secretion during recovery sleep, demonstrates endogenous DSIP is necessary (not just sufficient) for sleep-related GH release, animal study (rat intraventricular) — human mechanism extrapolated, establishes DSIP as part of GH secretion regulatory circuit during sleep]
- relevance: Links DSIP to growth hormone regulation — the primary recovery hormone released during slow-wave sleep. DSIP and ipamorelin operate complementary channels: DSIP promotes the sleep stage; ipamorelin amplifies the GH pulse. See Exercise Science section for GH recovery chain.
- crossReferences: ["exercise-protocols"]

**MOTS-c (2 studies):**

Study motsc-1: PMID 25738459, Lee C et al. 2015, Cell Metab

- year: 2015

- relevance: Foundational discovery establishing MOTS-c as mitochondria-to-muscle signaling peptide — directly connecting to metabolic optimization in Nutrition Science (AMPK activation, mitochondrial biogenesis, raising BMR). See Nutrition Science section.
- crossReferences: ["nutrition-science"]

**5-Amino-1MQ (2 studies):**

Study 5amino1mq-1: PMID 29155147, Neelakantan H et al. 2018, Biochem Pharmacol

- crossReferences: ["nutrition-science"]

- year: 2019
- summary: NNMT inhibition in 24-month-old (aged) mice activated dormant muscle stem cells post-injury, increasing cross-sectional area ~2-fold and peak torque ~70%, via SIRT1 activity restoration through NAD+ preservation.
- keyFindings: [activated senescent muscle stem cells in aged mice post-injury, ~2-fold greater muscle fiber cross-sectional area vs untreated aged controls, ~70% improvement in peak torque (strength) post-injury, mechanism: preserved NAD+ salvage pathway restoring SIRT1 sirtuin activity, aged mouse model — relevant to sarcopenia prevention — human trials not published]

- crossReferences: ["exercise-protocols", "nutrition-science"]

**Co-op Sourcing (2 studies — place at end of studies array):**

Study coop-purity-1: PMID 22033292, Verbeken M et al. 2012, Anal Biochem
- title: "The influence of peptide impurity profiles on functional tissue-organ bath response: the 11-mer peptide INSL6[151-161] case"
- authors: "Verbeken M, Wynendaele E, Lefebvre RA, et al."
- journal: "Analytical Biochemistry"
- year: 2012
- summary: Analytical chemistry study demonstrating crude peptide (~70% purity) produced strong contractile responses in biological tissue, while highly purified peptide (>=95%) showed no effect — proving synthesis by-products create false positive biological results.
